## Post 1 — Expertise signal (pure value, no pitch)
### Send: Week 1, Day 1

---

Most people look at the bank statements first.

That's the wrong place.

When I'm investigating a suspected bookkeeper fraud case, the first document I pull is the supplier ledger.

Here's why.

Bookkeeper fraud almost always runs through suppliers — not through direct cash withdrawals. Fictitious vendors. Inflated invoices. Split payments deliberately kept below approval thresholds so they never hit a manager's desk.

The bank statement shows money leaving. It doesn't tell you where it went or why without cross-referencing against who got paid.

The supplier ledger tells you that. Then you check those suppliers against Companies House. Then against the contract. Then against the approval chain.

In most small businesses, that entire process takes under two hours.

The businesses that catch it early are the ones where someone thought to ask those questions before the MD called in a panic.

Most don't. Most find out when the bookkeeper's already gone.

If you work in finance, compliance, or IT security for an SME — this is the kind of thing that's worth knowing before you need it.

### Lynsey Post 1 — Expertise signal
*(Send: Day 2–3, between Paul's Post 1 and Post 2)*

---

The fraud wasn't in the accounts.

It was in the accounts payable process.

A client of mine had a bookkeeper who'd been with them for six years. Trusted. Never a query. Always on top of it.

Over 18 months, she'd created three fictitious suppliers. All registered at residential addresses. All passing basic due diligence because nobody checked at the time.

The invoices were always under the MD's approval threshold. Never quite big enough to require a second sign-off.

Total loss before it was caught: just over £40,000.

What found it wasn't an audit. It was a new finance manager who noticed two supplier names were formatted the same unusual way and got curious.

The red flag was hiding in plain sight. It just needed someone who knew what to look for.

That's what forensic accounting training actually is — learning to notice what doesn't look right before someone has to call the police.
